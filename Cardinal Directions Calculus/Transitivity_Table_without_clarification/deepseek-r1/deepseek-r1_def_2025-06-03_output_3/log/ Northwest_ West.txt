ANSWER:

- Region \(x\) is **Northwest** of region \(y\): This implies \(x\) is both north and west of \(y\).
- Region \(y\) is **West** of region \(z\): This implies \(y\) is directly west of \(z\), with no significant north/south displacement relative to \(z\).

The vector from \(z\) to \(x\) is the sum of the vector from \(z\) to \(y\) (westward) and the vector from \(y\) to \(x\) (northwestward). Thus, \(x\) is:
- North of \(z\) (due to the north component from \(y\) to \(x\)).
- West of \(z\) (due to the west components from both \(z\) to \(y\) and \(y\) to \(x\)).

The exact direction depends on the magnitudes of these displacements:
- If the northward displacement dominates significantly, \(x\) could be **North** of \(z\).
- If the westward and northward displacements are comparable, \(x\) could be **Northwest** of \(z\).
- If the westward displacement dominates significantly, \(x\) could be **West** of \(z\).

Therefore, the possible relations between \(x\) and \(z\) are:
- **North**
- **Northwest**
- **West**

These cover the scenarios where \(x\) is generally northwest of \(z\), but the specific relation varies based on the relative distances. Other directions (e.g., Northeast, East, Southwest) are not possible as they would require \(x\) to be east or south of \(z\), which contradicts the given relations.
