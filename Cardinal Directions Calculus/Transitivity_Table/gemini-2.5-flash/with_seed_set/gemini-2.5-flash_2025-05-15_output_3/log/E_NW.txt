PROMPT: If region x is E of region y and region y is NW of region z, then which could be the possible relations between region x and region z?

ANSWER:

Let the bounding box of a region $a$ be denoted by $MBB(a) = [infx(a), supx(a)] \times [infy(a), supy(a)]$.
Regions in REG are homeomorphic to a closed unit disk, which implies that for any region $a \in REG$, $infx(a) < supx(a)$ and $infy(a) < supy(a)$.

The given information is:
1.  $x$ E $y$: $supx(y) \le infx(x)$, $infy(y) \le infy(x)$, and $supy(x) \le supy(y)$.
2.  $y$ NW $z$: $supx(z) \le infx(y)$ and $supy(z) \le infy(y)$.

We want to find the possible relations between $x$ and $z$. Let's analyze the constraints on their bounding box coordinates.

From (1), we have:
$infx(x) \ge supx(y)$
$infy(x) \ge infy(y)$
$supy(x) \le supy(y)$

From (2), we have:
$infx(y) \ge supx(z)$
$infy(y) \ge supy(z)$

Combining the inequalities for the x-coordinates:
$infx(x) \ge supx(y)$. Since $y \in REG$, $supx(y) > infx(y)$. So, $infx(x) > infx(y)$.
$infx(y) \ge supx(z)$. Since $z \in REG$, $supx(z) > infx(z)$. So, $infx(y) > infx(z)$.
Combining these, $infx(x) > infx(y) \ge supx(z) > infx(z)$.
This gives us $infx(x) > supx(z)$ and $infx(x) > infx(z)$.

Combining the inequalities for the y-coordinates:
$infy(x) \ge infy(y)$
$infy(y) \ge supy(z)$. Since $z \in REG$, $supy(z) > infy(z)$. So, $infy(y) > infy(z)$.
Combining these, $infy(x) \ge infy(y) \ge supy(z) > infy(z)$.
This gives us $infy(x) \ge supy(z)$ and $infy(x) > infy(z)$.

Now let's consider the upper bounds.
$supx(x) > infx(x)$. We know $infx(x) > supx(z)$. So, $supx(x) > infx(x) > supx(z)$.
This gives us $supx(x) > supx(z)$.

$supy(x) \le supy(y)$.
$supy(y) > infy(y)$.
$infy(y) \ge supy(z)$.
So, $supy(x) \le supy(y) > infy(y) \ge supy(z)$.
This implies $supy(x) \le supy(y)$ and $supy(y) > supy(z)$. This does not directly constrain $supy(x)$ relative to $supy(z)$.

Let's use the vertical containment from $x$ E $y$: $infy(y) \le infy(x)$ and $supy(x) \le supy(y)$.
Combined with $supy(z) \le infy(y)$, we get the chain:
$supy(z) \le infy(y) \le infy(x) \le supy(x) \le supy(y)$.

From this chain, we have:
$supy(z) \le infy(x)$ (This is one of the conditions for NE).
$supy(z) \le supy(x)$.

Can $supy(z) = supy(x)$? If $supy(z) = supy(x)$, then from the chain, $supy(z) = infy(y) = infy(x) = supy(x)$.
If $infy(x) = supy(x)$, then the region $x$ is a horizontal line segment, which is not in REG.
Therefore, $infy(x) < supy(x)$, which implies $supy(z) < supy(x)$.
So, $supy(x) > supy(z)$.

Summarizing the derived necessary conditions on the bounding boxes of $x$ and $z$:
$infx(x) > supx(z)$
$infy(x) \ge supy(z)$ (Equality is possible if $infy(x) = infy(y) = supy(z)$)
$supx(x) > supx(z)$
$supy(x) > supy(z)$

Let's check which cardinal direction relations between $x$ and $z$ are consistent with these necessary conditions.
The definition of $a$ NE $b$ is $supx(b) \le infx(a)$ and $supy(b) \le infy(a)$.
For $x$ NE $z$: $supx(z) \le infx(x)$ and $supy(z) \le infy(x)$.
Our derived conditions $infx(x) > supx(z)$ and $infy(x) \ge supy(z)$ satisfy these inequalities.
Thus, NE is a possible relation.
